Mechanical

Tower

Overview of dimensions

The construction of the tower began with looking up actual dimensions of the tower, and then scaling it. Figure 1 below shows a simple diagram which labels the dimensions we thought would be relevant for us to construct a scaled model:


Figure 1: A labeled tower to show the necessary dimensions

Because we wanted to make the tower as large as possible, we explored scaling to 6 inches of diameter, or 30 inches of height. However, we soon found that our servo motors could not support the weight of too tall a tower (the taller the tower, the more torque the servo needs to overcome to tilt the tower). So, we came to size the tower with 6 inches of diameter, resulting in around 22 inches of height.

Below, you can see the complete scaling, with the original dimensions included for reference:


Figure 2: A table of the tower dimensions

*The tower tilts, and so the opening cut into the top interface of the game will be a rectangle with semicircular sides. We assumed that the extreme case scenario would be when the tower was tilted at 45 degrees (the maximum that a servo motor could do), and so found the necessary dimension based on such a tilt. The dimension “Max dia” refers to the major diameter of said almost-elliptical shape.

**some of the dimensions, such as h1 were not used for the construction of our tower, but were left in the table to foster a sense of appreciation for the original architectural wonder.

Material Considerations

Given our tower dimensions, the next step was to decide how to make it. While we considered many options ranging from Duron to acrylic, we found the “best” to be foam core. Each layer of the tower had a foam core piece, and were connected to each other via 4 quarter inch dowels. In order to make the tower even lighter, we cut out the middle portion of each layer. This had the additional functionality of allowing us to pass the wires for the LEDs through, and to tape the LED down to each layer.

Final Tower

Thus, the tower is shown in the CAD rendering as below, in Figure 3.

All the layers were made in foam core, after discovering even the thinnest acrylic pieces were too heavy. The base of the tower in made in Duron, as it sits directly on the motor, and we felt that it could use some extra material strength.

The close up view shows how the base of the tower as a small hole in the middle – this is used by the motor that shakes the tower. There are also 4 small holes in the shape of a rectangle. These holes were used to hold down a DC motor, which worked to raise and lower a ball which would constitute our timing mechanism.

In our final design, the base also carried a pulley, which had string wrapped around it, and the top layer also had a pulley in the middle. They worked with the DC motor to bring the ball up and down.


Figure 3: A rendered CAD image of the final tower

Future Direction

Upon further consideration, it is now apparent to us that not every layer necessarily needed to have a foam core base. We were covering up the outside of the tower anyways, so realistically, a piece at the base to hold the motors, a piece at the top to give it a ceiling, and another piece for the smaller diameter ceiling to aesthetic purposes would have been sufficient.

Game Interface

Given the limited space, we focused on making the game interface a flat surface, with the tower standing up from it. The first part of the game involves covering three tape sensors. To indicate this to our users, we decided to embed the sensors in a slightly lowered surface that is in the shape of three hands. We thought it would be quite natural for people to put their hands where they saw hands.

We also left a rectangular cut out for the LCD screen and the keypad, and an elliptical cut out for the tower. In addition, there were multiple circular cut outs for the LEDs and the potentiometer knob to stick through.

The top view of the interface is shown in Figure 4.

Note that the keypad covering is a separate material, because we wanted to emphasize that the keypad was a separate part, and so we felt that using a completely different material would help to make sure the user noticed it more easily.


Figure 4: The top interface of the game, with labeled cut-outs

Key to Disarmament, Key to Success

One of the more aesthetic part of our game revolved around the key. Our idea for a big motion was to have a giant key moved. Of course, given that the DDM had to fit within an 18 by 18 by 36 inch volume, the key naturally couldn’t be too large. In the end, our key turned out as shown in Figure 5.


Figure 5: The key

The key itself had a maximum height of nearly 8 inches. So it was rather sizeable.

We wanted the key to be moved from one location to the next during the game. However, this ran into the problem of how we should “reset” after one user was done, and what a logical way to communicate this motion would be. We decided that if a key rotated, and was unlocked, it would be natural for the user to pull it out, and subsequently replace it in another key-hole. Thus came our idea of having two key holes. This way, we can have a sensor, and the Tiva would respond if there were a change in the sensor state – indicate that the key had moved.

It was simple matter to cut out the key-hole, and to paint the circular area around it a separate color to make it very clearly different. But the problem was how to lock the key.

We overcame this situation by rotating the key in the hole, but leaving the hole unmoved. That way, when the key was perpendicular to the slit, it could not be removed.

In the Figure 6 below, we see the internal structure of the game interface. The key sits in a pocket made so that the key can really only be inserted vertically, and this pocket sits a top of a circular disc which gets turned by a servo. The key pockets also have a small hole in it, which allows a tape sensor to sit in it, and sense whether the key is there or not.

In the figure on the left we see that the key could not come out of the slit, but in the right figure, the key had been rotated 90 degrees, and so it is free to be pulled out.


Figure 6: The keys in locked and unlocked states

Also, note the other keyhole. In Key2a, the other key is also locked, and in Key2b, the second key hole is also unlocked. They two key holes are perpendicular to each other because we thought that it would be more exciting for the user if there were a bit of rotation in the key orientation. The servo motors are controlled together here, again reducing the number of Tiva pins we need to control.

In reality, the circular disks with the key-holes were attached to the top interface, but we thought this would be a better way to communicate the idea.
